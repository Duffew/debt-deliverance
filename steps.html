Behaviour

One of the hardest parts of my own journey to financial freedom was fully understanding that it was my responsibility. My background didn't matter, my upbringing didn't matter, my social class didn't matter, my gender didn't matter; nothing mattered other than my behaviour. The decisions that I had made, which then manifested as actions in the world, had put me in debt. Nothing else. My behaviour was responsible for my circumstances and I was responsible for my behaviour. At that moment, my debt became the symptom, not the problem. My REAL problem was that my behaviour was bad. If I wanted to be free of debt, then I needed better behaviour, and so do you.

principles

In order to guide your behaviour, you must adopt a set principles to provide you with direction when things get tough. There will be times when you will need to make hard choices and good decisions. Reactive decisions, taken in the heat of the moment and based upon what feels good at the time, are rarely good ones. Decisions made proactivly from a point of principle, are usully better, provided the principles are sound.


So what might a set of principles for getting out of debt look like? You may want to develop your own over time as you learn to manage your money, but to get you started, the following may be helpful:


  • Do not borrow money, ever

    If you don't have the money to pay for somthing in full, then you can't afford it and you do not buy it. This means no borrowing on credit cards, no car loans, no student loans, no personal loans, no familly loans. The borrower is always slave to the lender and part of the stress you feel is the reality of this dynamic. You're here because you have debt. Stop borrowing money now. Cut up your credit cards and do not take a consolidation loan. More loans are not the solution to debt; changing your behaviour is.


  • Write it down

    To be sucessful with money, you must use a written budget. The budget should include: a clearly stated purpose at the top of the page; the timeframe it covers; your projected income over that timeframe; your essentail expenses then non-essential expenses; a list of any assets followed by a list of your debts. Then, subtract your projected expenses from your income to show that there is money left over. If there isn't, you either need to increase your income or cut your expenses, preferably both. A written budget serves two purposes. Firstly it shows you the reality of your financial situation, and secondly gives you accountability. The budget is your plan. You wrote it, now you stick to it.


  • Behaviour matters, be deliberate

    Most of the people I know who have or had debt, myself included, drifted into it. It wasn't planned ahead of time. More often than not, it was a childish reaction to life's events. FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out) or worry about what others might think prompt terrible financial decisions. 'My friend wants to get married in Bali and I have no money. What will my friend think of I say no? I'll put the trip on the credit card.' Or 'I can't turn up to work in an old car, what will my colleagues say? I'll take a loan on a new car.' When loans are on offer with almost any purchse in today's world, wandering in to debt, to placate our feelings, is very easy. You cannot, however, wander out. You must make a deliberate decision every day to live within your means. You will need to say 'No' to yourself and, far harder, you will need to say 'No' to your friends and family. This will be the toughest part, so be prepared and stick to your principles.


  • Get the sequence right

    Life is full of tasks that we need to complete. The order in which we do these tasks has a dramatic effect on the likelihood of a successful outcome. You don't put your shoes on before your socks because that would be silly. You follow the right, logical sequence and your feet have a comfortable day. Similarly, there is a logical sequence for paying off debt. Following the sequence, detailed below, will lead to success.



The above principles are a useful starting point, not only for managing debt, but also for building wealth after debt. They can be used to help you keep control of your finances throughout your lifetime and with consistant application can turn managing money into a satisfying activity rather than a sorrowful one.

process

Pre-requisites - marriages and civil partnerships:


  • You must have life insurance, particularly if you have children. You should also have insurance for your house, car and consider insurance for loss of income or long-term disability. You must protect yourself and those you love from financial loss in the event of the unexpected.
  • You will only do this successfully if you combine finances. Your debts are both your debts, and your incomes are both your incomes. Complete transparency and trust are required and to that end, one joint current account into which and from which all money flows. That doesn't mean you keep separate current accounts and each contribute to a joint account for the bills. One joint account only for everything.

Without these pre-requisites, your task will be immeasurably harder. If you are married or in a civil partnership, you must work together and provision for each other if one of you is incapacitated or lost. The following sequence should be taken together and in complete agreement.


steps for getting out of debt

  1. Choose a start date

    You will need a few hours to work through these steps so agree a date and time when you are able to focus on just this. My wife and I set aside Sunday mornings for a family meeting every week where we discuss everything from finances to who's cooking what for dinner on which day. Go somewhere outside the house so the temptation to 'just put the laundry on' is removed. If you have young children, ask someone look after them for a couple of hours. You need to get used to being deliberate about managing your money. Set time aside for it.
  2. Write a vision statement - this is your written statement about why you want to be out of debt. It is a vision of what life looks like without debt. It could be you want to stop worrying about the bills; you want to reduce stress levels so you can enjoy time with the people you love; you want to build long-term wealth to provide for a dignified retirement. Whatever the reason for tackling your debt, it needs to be larger than the debt itself and worthwhile enough to keep you moving towards it. It doesn't need to be detailed, just a sentence or two but it needs to be written down and agreed.
  3. Cut up your credit cards - this will be harder to do than it seems as our emotional attachment to these small pieces of plastic can be very strong, but is an important practical and symbolic step. You should also remove any credit card information stored in your web browser making the 'One Click Purchase' much harder. From this moment, you buy nothing unless you have the money to pay for it in your current account. This is going to help you develop good habits by encourage you to keep a close eye on your bank account and check that you have the money before you buy things.
  4. List all your debts - write down every debt you have starting with smallest at the top of the page and the largest at the bottom. Include the type, company owed, amount and monthly payment but exclude your mortgage. Then total up the full amount outstanding and the total monthly payment. This is the full scope of your battle. The way to win is to focus your attention on the smallest debt first. Pay the minimum on everything else but get the smallest paid off quickly and the feeling of success will provide positive reinforcement for your good behaviour and propel you onto the next fight. By the time you hit the bottom of the page, momentum and renewed self-belief will make short work of your largest debt. The sequence here is important. Don't go for the highest interest rate first or the largest monthly payment first. Start with the smallest debt and get some quick wins.
  5. List all your assets - make a list of everything you own that has a cash value. Include cash in bank accounts, investment accounts, cars, precious metals, expensive electronics, bicycles, and anything else that you might be able to sell, but do not include any private pensions. You need to keep £1,000 aside in a savings account for a basic emergency fund, but beyond that, you sell everything that is not essential. If you don't have any cash at all, then you are selling things to raise £1,000 first. You will likely find selling off assets difficult. If you do, compare your list of assets with your Vision Statement. Put them side by side. Which is more important? If it's the asset list, you need a bigger vision.
  6. Write your budget - Writing a budget is a skill that takes time to develop so don't worry if you find this difficult at first. The more you do it, the better you'll get. The key is that once it is written, you stick to it. The budget is your planned spending for the month based upon your projected income for the month. To write the budget, start at the top of the page with your estimated income for that month. Next, list your essential expenses only followed by 3 columns titled ESTIMATE, ACTUAL and BALANCE and assign each category an amount in the ESTIMATE column. The total of the ESTIMATE column is equal to your income. The categories for essential expenses are: food and household, utilities, rent or mortgage, council tax, transportation and debt payments. Whilst you are in debt, these are the only line items you need. You are not taking holidays. You are not eating in restaurants or going to the pub. You are not going to the cinema or buying expensive clothes. There are no non-essential expenses. You are on a subsistence lifestyle until the debt is gone. Track your spending and take a few minutes at the end of every day to update your ACTUAL and BALANCE columns on your budget ensuring that you stay within your ESTIMATE. Do a weekly review of your progress and write a new budget each month.
  7. Get to work - Cancel your streaming subscriptions and pick up an evening job, you need the extra income to bring forward your debt free date and you won't be having evenings out. Pick up some shifts in a pub or restaurant; deliver pizza or drive for Uber. Do something to bring in extra money. Its only for a short time but while you have debt your life should consist of working, sleeping and paying off debt and that is it. Keep your Vision centred in your mind and get to it.

The above steps are your blueprint for freeing yourself from debt and laying the foundations for building wealth. The steps represent a principles-based approach to changing your behaviour, that, if followed will give you financial peace of mind throughout your life. My part was to outline the steps and the principles behind them in order to give you hope that you can take control of your life. Your part is do it and you can! Get to work!
